[S2]: Photosynthesis is the process by which plants, some bacteria, and certain protists convert sunlight into chemical energy. The main ingredients are carbon dioxide and water, and the end products are glucose and oxygen. The overall equation is carbon dioxide plus water yields glucose plus oxygen.  

[S1]: And that’s all thanks to chlorophyll, right?  

[S2]: Right. Chlorophyll is the green pigment in plants that captures light energy. There are different types, like chlorophyll a and b, and accessory pigments like carotenoids. These pigments absorb light at different wavelengths, which is why plants appear green—they reflect green light.  

[S1]: So once the light is absorbed, what happens next?  

[S2]: Great question. The process is split into two main stages: the light-dependent reactions and the light-independent reactions. The light-dependent reactions happen in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplasts.  

[S1]: And that’s where the energy from light is used to split water into oxygen, hydrogen ions, and electrons.  

[S2]: Exactly. This process is called photolysis. The electrons then move through an electron transport chain, which helps pump hydrogen ions into the thylakoid, creating a concentration gradient. This gradient is used to produce ATP through a process called chemiosmosis.  

[S1]: And ATP is the energy currency of the cell, right?  

[S2]: Yes. Alongside NADPH, which is another energy carrier, ATP is used in the second stage of photosynthesis—the light-independent reactions, also known as the Calvin Cycle.  

[S1]: So the Calvin Cycle is where carbon dioxide is fixed into glucose?  

[S2]: That’s right. It takes place in the stroma of the chloroplast. Carbon dioxide combines with a five-carbon molecule called RuBP, forming an unstable six-carbon compound that quickly breaks down into two three-carbon molecules. These are then converted into glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate, or G3P.  

[S1]: And some of that G3P is used to make glucose, while the rest is recycled to keep the cycle going.  

[S2]: Exactly. And the whole process is powered by the ATP and NADPH from the light-dependent reactions.  

[S1]: What about the factors that affect the rate of photosynthesis?  

[S2]: The main ones are light intensity, carbon dioxide concentration, and temperature. At first, increasing any of these will increase the rate of photosynthesis, but eventually, another factor becomes the limiting one. For example, if there’s plenty of light but not enough carbon dioxide, the rate will plateau.  

[S1]: And chlorophyll plays a role in how much light is absorbed.  

[S2]: Definitely. Chlorophyll a absorbs light most efficiently at 680 and 700 nanometers, which is why those wavelengths are most effective for photosynthesis.
